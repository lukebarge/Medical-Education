Action Potential

A rapid, self-propagating reversal of membrane potential that carries information along neurons and triggers contraction in muscle cells.

action-potential.js — live simulation

Overview

At rest, a neuron maintains a resting membrane potential of around −70 mV, kept by the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump and selective permeability of ion channels. When a sufficiently large stimulus depolarises the membrane to the threshold (approximately −55 mV), an all-or-nothing action potential is triggered.

💡
All-or-nothing principle If the stimulus is below threshold, no action potential fires. Above threshold, the response is identical regardless of how strong the stimulus is. Adjust the threshold slider to see this principle in action.

Phases

📉
Resting (−70 mV)
High K⁺ inside, high Na⁺ outside. Na⁺/K⁺ pump maintains gradient. Membrane is polarised.
⬆️
Depolarisation
Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open rapidly. Na⁺ rushes in; membrane potential spikes to +40 mV.
⬇️
Repolarisation
Na⁺ channels inactivate. Voltage-gated K⁺ channels open; K⁺ flows out, restoring negativity.
🔒
Refractory Period
Absolute: Na⁺ channels inactivated; no new AP possible. Relative: stronger stimulus required.

Parameters

ParameterTypical ValueWhat it changes
Threshold −55 mV Membrane potential at which voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open. Local anaesthetics raise threshold.
Conduction Velocity 1× (relative) Speed of impulse propagation. Increased in large-diameter and myelinated (saltatory) fibres.
Refractory Period 2 ms Time until another AP can be triggered. Limits maximum firing rate to ~500 Hz.

Clinical Relevance

Local anaesthetics (e.g., lidocaine) block voltage-gated Na⁺ channels, raising the threshold and preventing action potential generation — hence abolishing pain without affecting consciousness. Tetrodotoxin from puffer fish is a potent Na⁺ channel blocker.

Multiple sclerosis destroys myelin sheaths, dramatically slowing conduction velocity as saltatory conduction fails. Increasing the refractory period also models the effect of some anti-epileptic drugs that reduce neuronal firing rate.

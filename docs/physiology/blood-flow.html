Blood Flow

Blood circulates continuously through systemic and pulmonary circuits, driven by the pressure gradient generated by the heart.

blood-flow.js — live simulation

Overview

Blood flow obeys an analogy to Ohm's Law: Q = ΔP / R, where Q is flow (cardiac output), ΔP is the pressure difference between the arterial and venous sides, and R is total peripheral resistance. Increasing resistance or decreasing pressure reduces the flow of blood to tissues.

The simulation shows oxygenated (arterial) blood flowing from the heart through the systemic circuit, and deoxygenated (venous) blood returning via the pulmonary circuit.

ℹ️
Ohm's Law analogy Flow = Pressure ÷ Resistance. A twofold increase in vascular resistance halves blood flow at the same pressure — unless the heart compensates by working harder.

Determinants of Blood Flow

🔴
Cardiac Output
Heart Rate × Stroke Volume. Average 5 L/min at rest; up to 25 L/min during vigorous exercise.
🩸
Vascular Resistance
Primarily controlled by arteriolar smooth muscle tone. Increases with vasoconstriction, arterial stiffness, or obstructive plaques.
💧
Blood Viscosity
Increases with haematocrit (polycythaemia) and decreases with anaemia. Elevated viscosity raises resistance.

Clinical Relevance

Hypertension reflects chronically elevated driving pressure, often accompanied by increased peripheral resistance. Over time it damages vessel walls and forces the heart to work harder, leading to hypertrophy and eventual failure.

Haemorrhagic shock drops blood pressure, reducing flow to vital organs. The body compensates by increasing heart rate and vasoconstricting peripheral vessels to maintain central perfusion — set a low pressure and high resistance to observe this redistributive pattern.

Cardiac Cycle

The sequence of mechanical and electrical events that drive one complete heartbeat — from atrial contraction to ventricular relaxation.

heart-beat.js — live simulation

Overview

The cardiac cycle encompasses all events from the beginning of one heartbeat to the beginning of the next. At a resting rate of 72 beats per minute, one complete cycle lasts approximately 0.8 seconds.

The cycle is divided into two main phases: systole, during which the ventricles contract and eject blood; and diastole, during which the ventricles relax and fill with blood returning from the lungs and body.

ℹ️
Frank–Starling mechanism The more the ventricles are stretched during diastole, the more forcefully they contract during systole. Adjust the Sarcomere Length slider to see this effect — a longer sarcomere (greater pre-load) generates stronger contraction.

Phases of the Cycle

📥
Ventricular Filling
Blood flows passively from atria into relaxed ventricles. Ends with atrial kick (~20% extra filling).
⚡
Isovolumetric Contraction
Ventricles contract but all valves are closed. Pressure rises sharply with no change in volume.
🩸
Ventricular Ejection
Semilunar valves open. Stroke volume (~70 mL) is expelled into aorta and pulmonary artery.
🔓
Isovolumetric Relaxation
Ventricles relax, valves close. Pressure drops rapidly until AV valves open and filling restarts.

Parameters

Parameter Normal Range What it changes
Heart Rate 60 – 100 bpm Cycle duration. Tachycardia (>100) shortens diastolic filling time.
Sarcomere Length 2.0 – 2.4 μm Pre-load. Longer fibres overlap optimally → stronger contraction (Frank–Starling).
Contractility 0.5 – 1.0 Inotropic state. Increased by sympathetic activation, catecholamines.

Clinical Relevance

Alterations in the cardiac cycle underlie many common conditions. Heart failure reduces contractility, lowering stroke volume and cardiac output. Atrial fibrillation eliminates the atrial kick, reducing filling by up to 20% — significant in patients who rely on it. Aortic stenosis increases afterload, prolonging the ejection phase and causing compensatory hypertrophy.

Vascular Resistance & Cardiac Output

A physics simulation of the left ventricle pumping blood against systemic vascular resistance — linking Ohm's law, Frank–Starling mechanics, and Poiseuille's law into one interactive model.

vascular-resistance.js — live simulation

Overview

The cardiovascular system obeys the same Ohm's law analogy that governs electrical circuits: MAP = CO × SVR, where mean arterial pressure (MAP) is the product of cardiac output (CO) and systemic vascular resistance (SVR). Raise resistance without a compensatory increase in heart rate or contractility, and pressure climbs — the hallmark of hypertension.

The simulation models pulsatile flow, showing how the left ventricle generates an aortic pressure waveform with each beat. Blood particles (red = arterial, blue = venous) flow at a rate proportional to cardiac output, while the arteriole width reflects Poiseuille's law: resistance scales with the fourth power of vessel radius, so even small changes in arteriolar tone produce dramatic effects on flow.

ℹ️
Frank–Starling & afterload Increasing SVR raises the afterload against which the left ventricle must eject. According to Frank–Starling mechanics, a higher afterload reduces stroke volume — watch the SV readout fall as you increase SVR. The heart compensates partly via reflex tachycardia (increase HR) to maintain cardiac output.

Physics Model

⚡
Ohm's Law Analogy
MAP = CO × SVR. Pressure difference drives flow; resistance opposes it. Identical in form to V = I × R.
🩸
Poiseuille's Law
R = 8ηL / πr⁴. Halving arteriole radius increases resistance 16-fold. The simulation narrows vessels visually as SVR rises.
📈
Frank–Starling
Higher afterload (SVR) reduces stroke volume. The model: SV = EDV × EF × [1 / (0.7 + 0.3 × SVR)].
🌊
Aortic Pressure Waveform
Systole generates a rapid pressure rise to SBP. Diastolic runoff decays exponentially toward DBP via aortic compliance.

Parameters

Parameter Normal Range Effect
SVR (Vascular Resistance) ×0.5 – ×3.0 Controls arteriole tone. ↑SVR → ↑MAP, ↓SV, narrower arterioles. Mimics vasoconstrictors, hypertension.
Heart Rate 40 – 150 bpm ↑HR compensates for ↓SV to maintain CO = HR × SV. Tachycardia is a baroreflex response to low MAP.
Contractility 0.3 – 2.0 Inotropic state. ↑Contractility → ↑EF → ↑SV → ↑CO → ↑MAP. Mimics catecholamines or inotropes.
Preload 0.5 – 1.8 End-diastolic volume. ↑Preload → ↑EDV → ↑SV (Frank–Starling length-tension effect).

Clinical Scenarios

Use the sliders to recreate common haemodynamic states:

  • Hypertension: SVR ×2–3, normal CO — MAP rises, pulse pressure widens.
  • Cardiogenic shock: ↓Contractility → ↓SV → ↓CO → ↓MAP. Baroreflex: ↑HR tries to compensate.
  • Septic shock (distributive): SVR ×0.3–0.5, ↑HR — low MAP despite preserved or elevated CO.
  • Vasodilator therapy: ↓SVR → afterload relief → ↑SV (useful in heart failure).
  • Exercise: ↑HR + ↑Contractility + mild ↓SVR → large ↑CO, modest ↑MAP.

Drug Absorption

The process by which drug molecules move from the site of administration into the systemic bloodstream, governed by route, physicochemistry, and physiology.

drug-absorption.js — live simulation

Overview

Absorption is the first step in the pharmacokinetic process — before a drug can reach its target it must cross biological membranes and enter the circulation. The fraction of an administered dose that reaches the systemic circulation is the bioavailability (F), expressed as a percentage.

Switch between routes using the buttons in the simulation. Observe how the particle pathway and the absorbed fraction change with each route.

Routes of Administration

💊
Oral (PO)
Most convenient. Drug traverses GI epithelium and portal circulation before reaching systemic blood. Subject to first-pass metabolism.
💉
Intravenous (IV)
100% bioavailability by definition. Rapid onset. No absorption barrier. Used in emergencies and for drugs with poor oral bioavailability.
👅
Sublingual (SL)
Absorbed through oral mucosa directly into venous drainage. Bypasses first-pass effect. Rapid onset — used for glyceryl trinitrate.
⚠️
First-pass effect After oral absorption, drugs pass through the portal vein to the liver before reaching systemic circulation. Extensive hepatic metabolism here reduces bioavailability — for example, morphine has ~25% oral bioavailability due to first-pass metabolism.

Clinical Relevance

Understanding bioavailability is essential when switching between routes. Converting a patient from IV morphine to oral requires a dose increase of approximately 3× to compensate for first-pass metabolism.

Drugs with very low oral bioavailability (e.g., vancomycin ~0%) must be given IV for systemic effect, though oral vancomycin is used intentionally to treat Clostridioides difficile infection in the GI lumen.
